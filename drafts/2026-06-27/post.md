## Headnote

Morocco sits at the hinge of two culinary worlds, and a tagine makes that geography edible. What reads as an exotic North African dish is, in structure, a straight French braise: fat, aromatics, protein, liquid, low heat, time. The difference is in the spice logic — turmeric and ginger instead of thyme and bay, preserved lemon instead of a vinegar deglaze, olives adding brine where a French cook might reach for capers. Understanding that parallel is what lets you make this recipe in a Dutch oven on a Paris gas ring or in a clay tagine on a Moroccan charcoal brazier and get the same result either way. The beef here is lean, diced, and cooked until it yields without falling apart. The potatoes absorb the spiced cooking liquid. The olives and preserved lemon go in at the end, because heat makes olives bitter and destroys the clean acidity of the lemon peel.

## Ingredients

### Spice paste and aromatics
- 1 large onion (about 200 g / 7 oz), thinly sliced
- 3 cloves garlic, grated on a Microplane or crushed to a paste
- 1 tsp ground ginger
- 1 tsp ground turmeric
- ½ tsp ground black pepper
- 2 tsp fine salt, plus more to finish
- 1 pinch saffron threads (optional but recommended)
- 80 ml (⅓ cup) extra-virgin olive oil

### Main
- 500 g (about 1 lb) lean halal beef, cut into 4–5 cm (1½–2 in) cubes
- 700 g (1 lb 9 oz) waxy potatoes, peeled and cut into thick wedges
- 700 ml (3 cups) water

### Finishing
- 1 large handful green or red olives, pitted or unpitted
- ½ preserved lemon, quartered lengthways, flesh scraped away, rind only
- 1 small handful flat-leaf parsley or coriander, roughly chopped
- 1 green cayenne chilli (optional)

## Ingredient Notes

**Preserved lemon.** Buy it; do not substitute fresh lemon zest. The fermentation changes the structure and flavour entirely — it becomes soft, almost jammy, and its bitterness disappears. Find it in any North African grocery and most French supermarkets (Monoprix stocks it reliably). Use only the rind; the flesh is extremely salty and will unbalance the dish.

**Saffron.** A pinch here means roughly 8–10 threads. Dissolve them in 2 tablespoons of the measured water for 10 minutes before using. This is not decoration — saffron released into fat rather than water behaves differently; dissolving in warm water extracts colour and flavour more efficiently when the liquid quantity is small.

**Beef cut.** The original recipe specifies lean beef. Chuck (paleron in French) works well and is inexpensive. Avoid anything too lean — topside will tighten in a braise this short. If you use a tougher secondary cut like shin, increase the braising time to 90 minutes minimum and add an extra 200 ml water.

**Olives.** Green olives hold their shape and stay firm; red (riper) olives are softer and their flavour is less aggressive. Either works. Avoid pre-marinated olives with herbs — they muddy the spice profile.

## Method

1. **Make the spice base.** In the base of your tagine or Dutch oven, combine the olive oil, sliced onion, garlic, ginger, turmeric, black pepper, salt, and dissolved saffron water. Stir to coat. This is your braising paste — every piece of protein and vegetable will be flavoured from this foundation.

2. **Add the beef.** Place the beef cubes directly into the spice-and-onion mixture. Turn each piece so it picks up colour from the turmeric. Do not brown the beef first. In a tagine braise, the goal is flavour integration, not the Maillard crust you'd want in a French daube. Browning here would also risk burning the turmeric before the liquid goes in.

3. **Layer the potatoes.** Arrange the potato wedges on top of and around the beef. Pour the water over everything. The liquid should come roughly halfway up the potatoes — not submerge them.

4. **Cook, covered, on low heat.** Place the lid on. Cook over low-medium heat for 40 minutes. If using a clay tagine on a gas hob, use a heat diffuser — direct flame on cold clay is how you crack it. If using a Dutch oven, medium-low is fine. Do not lift the lid for the first 30 minutes; the condensation cycling inside is doing work.

5. **Check doneness.** At 40 minutes, a knife should meet the beef with no resistance. The potatoes should be fully cooked through but holding their shape — they should not be falling apart. If the beef is not yet tender, cover and cook another 10 minutes, adding a splash of water if the base looks dry.

6. **Add the olives and preserved lemon.** Nestle the olives into the stew and lay the preserved lemon rind strips across the top. Cover and cook for a final 5 minutes. This is enough to warm them through and let the lemon's salt and acidity begin to bleed into the surrounding liquid — no more, or the olives turn bitter.

7. **Finish and serve.** Taste for salt. Scatter the chopped parsley or coriander over the top. If using the green chilli, lay it whole on top now — it adds fragrance more than heat at this stage. Serve directly from the tagine or pot.

## Variations

**Cheaper cut, longer braise.** Use bone-in beef shin (jarret de boeuf). Add 200 ml extra water, cook covered for 90 minutes on the lowest heat your hob will hold. The marrow from the bone enriches the sauce considerably.

**Lamb version.** Replace the beef with bone-in lamb shoulder pieces (about 600 g / 1 lb 5 oz). Reduce the water to 500 ml. Lamb releases more fat during cooking, so skim the surface before adding the olives at the end.

**Root vegetable swap.** Replace the potatoes with a combination of carrots and turnips cut into 4 cm pieces. Cook time stays the same. This makes the dish lighter and gives it a slightly sweeter base note.

**Without preserved lemon.** Add 1 tablespoon of capers, rinsed, and the zest of half an unwaxed lemon at the finishing stage. It is a different dish — the brine quality of the capers approximates the salt element, but the fermented depth of preserved lemon is absent. State this to your guests so expectations are calibrated.

## Storage and Reheating

Allow the tagine to cool fully before covering. Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to three days. The flavour improves overnight as the spices and the preserved lemon continue to meld.

To reheat: transfer to a saucepan with a splash of water (2–3 tablespoons), cover, and warm over low heat for 10 minutes, stirring once. Do not microwave the potatoes — they turn grainy. If you must microwave, pull the potatoes out, reheat them separately at 50% power.

Do not freeze — the potatoes will disintegrate on thawing. If you want to batch-cook, freeze the beef and sauce only (before adding potatoes and olives), then cook fresh potatoes when you reheat.

## FAQ

### Can I make this without a tagine?
Yes. A heavy-bottomed pot with a tight-fitting lid — a Dutch oven, a cocotte, or even a deep sauté pan with a lid that seals well — works identically. The clay tagine matters for presentation and for very long, charcoal-based cooking. On a domestic gas or electric hob, cast iron or enamelled cast iron holds heat more evenly.

### Do I need to marinate the beef?
Not for this recipe. The spices are cooked into the fat from the start, and the braise is long enough for full flavour penetration. Marinating would add little here and introduce extra steps. If you want to prep ahead, mix the beef with the spice paste and store it covered in the refrigerator overnight — this is not a marinade so much as seasoning in advance.

### My sauce looks too thin. How do I thicken it?
Uncover the pot for the last 10 minutes of cooking and increase the heat slightly. The starchy water released by the potatoes will reduce and thicken naturally. Do not add flour or cornstarch — it will make the sauce opaque and gluey, which is wrong for this style of dish.

### Can I use ground saffron instead of threads?
Ground saffron is more easily adulterated and harder to dose accurately. Use threads when you can. If you only have ground, use a very small pinch — roughly the amount that fits on the tip of a table knife — and dissolve it directly in the measured water.

### The preserved lemon rind is very salty. Should I rinse it?
Yes, briefly. A quick rinse under cold water removes surface salt without washing away the fermented flavour. If your preserved lemon is particularly salty, reduce the salt in the main recipe by ¼ tsp and adjust at the end.

### Is this dish spicy hot?
No, not as written. The cayenne chilli adds fragrance and mild warmth at most if left whole. To make it genuinely hot, slice the chilli and add it with the spice paste at the beginning. The ginger provides warmth but not capsaicin heat.

### Can I cook this in an oven instead of on the hob?
Yes. After assembling everything through step 3, cover and place in an oven preheated to 170°C (340°F) for 50 minutes. Check liquid levels at 35 minutes. The oven distributes heat more evenly than a hob flame and is less likely to scorch the base.
