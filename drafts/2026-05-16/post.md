## Headnote

Vadouvan is the French answer to a question nobody in France thought to ask. It arrived in Pondicherry with French colonists, absorbed shallots and curry leaf into its spice logic, and came back to France tasting like something that had always belonged there. It is sold in small jars at Épices Roellinger or Fauchon, and it makes almost anything smell magnificent the moment it hits fat.

This recipe pairs that spice mix with a semi-boneless leg of lamb: slow-roasted low and long, rubbed with a compound butter thick enough to stay on vertical surfaces, and built with orange zest and rosemary, which in combination with vadouvan produces something that sits somewhere between a French gigot and a Pakistani raan without fully being either. That is not a weakness. The butter bastes continuously as it melts, the low heat breaks down the tougher shoulder-adjacent meat, and the result slices like something far more technical than a spice rub and a roasting pan.

No alcohol, no pork, no ambiguity. This is halal French cooking done with full conviction.

## Ingredients

### Vadouvan butter
- 225 g (2 sticks / 8 oz) unsalted butter, softened to room temperature
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) vadouvan, or good-quality curry powder if unavailable
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) coarsely grated orange zest (from roughly 2 large oranges)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh rosemary, finely chopped
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) kosher salt
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) pink peppercorns, crushed in a mortar
- 5 ml (1 tsp) freshly ground black pepper

### Lamb and roasting
- 1 semi-boneless leg of lamb, shank bone in, approximately 3.8–4 kg (8½–9 lb)
- 240 ml (1 cup) water

## Ingredient Notes

**Vadouvan.** This is a French-colonial curry blend based on Tamil masala but finished with dried shallot, garlic, and often a touch of fenugreek. The dried alliums give it a deeper, slightly caramelized top note that plain curry powder does not have. In France it is widely available; elsewhere look online for Roellinger's or Burlap & Barrel's version. If substituting curry powder, add 5 ml (1 tsp) of granulated dried shallot or onion powder alongside to approximate that character.

**Pink peppercorns.** Not actually pepper. They are the dried berry of *Schinus terebinthifolia*, mild, slightly resinous, and more aromatic than hot. Crush them lightly so they remain visible and textural in the crust. They are common in French spice shops and online.

**Semi-boneless leg.** The shank bone remains in; the hip bone is removed. This is the standard butchery form sold in most halal butcher shops when you ask for a leg of lamb for roasting. It holds its shape, carves cleanly, and roasts evenly. Ask your butcher to score the surface for you if you prefer, though it is not required here.

**Butter.** Use full-fat unsalted. The salt is controlled separately. Clarifying the butter is unnecessary; the milk solids browning slightly at the edges of the roasting pan is part of the flavor.

## Method

1. **Make the vadouvan butter.** In a bowl, combine the softened butter, vadouvan, orange zest, rosemary, kosher salt, crushed pink peppercorns, and black pepper. Work everything together with a spatula or your hands until uniform. The butter will turn a deep ochre-orange. Set aside at room temperature. This can be made up to two days ahead and refrigerated; bring it back to spreadable temperature before using.

2. **Prepare the lamb.** Remove the lamb from the refrigerator 1 hour before roasting. Cold meat roasting straight from the fridge produces uneven cooking through the cross-section. Pat the surface dry with paper towels; moisture on the surface delays browning and makes the butter rub slip.

3. **Preheat the oven to 230°C (450°F).** You want a very hot oven at the start. This sets the crust and seals in the rub before the long slow phase begins.

4. **Apply the butter.** Place the lamb in a roasting pan large enough that there is at least 4 cm (1.5 in) of clearance around it. Pack the entire surface of the lamb with the compound butter, working it into any natural creases and around the shank bone. It should look aggressively coated, not lightly seasoned.

5. **Sear in the oven.** Roast uncovered at 230°C (450°F) for 25 minutes. The surface will develop a deep, fragrant crust and the butter will begin to pool and sizzle in the pan. This is correct.

6. **Reduce heat and add water.** Pull the pan out, lower the oven to 150°C (300°F), and pour the 240 ml of water into the bottom of the roasting pan, not over the lamb. The water creates steam that slows desiccation and mixes with the butter drippings into a self-basting medium. Return to the oven.

7. **Roast low and slow.** Continue roasting at 150°C (300°F) for approximately 2 hours 45 minutes to 3 hours, basting every 45 minutes with the pan juices. Target an internal temperature of 60°C (140°F) for pink, 68°C (155°F) for medium. A leg of lamb at this weight will carry over roughly 3–4°C after resting, so pull it just before your target.

8. **Rest the lamb.** Transfer to a cutting board and tent loosely with foil. Rest for a minimum of 20 minutes, preferably 30. The internal temperature will equalize across the joint and the juices will redistribute. Cutting too early wastes everything the long roast built.

9. **Finish the pan juices.** Tilt the roasting pan and skim the fat from the surface with a spoon, or use a fat separator. The remaining liquid is your sauce: concentrated, orange-spiced, lamby. Taste for salt. Serve it alongside, not poured over the sliced meat.

10. **Carve and serve.** Carve parallel to the bone in long, even slices. The meat near the shank will be the most well-done; slices from the central leg will be the most evenly pink. Arrange on a warm platter.

## Variations

**Shoulder instead of leg.** A bone-in lamb shoulder of 2–2.5 kg (4.5–5.5 lb) works beautifully with the same rub. Reduce the initial sear time to 20 minutes and the slow roast to 2 hours 15 minutes, or until the meat pulls away from the bone easily. Shoulder has more fat and connective tissue, so it goes more tender than sliceable, which is a different but equally valid result.

**Lamb shanks, individual portions.** Use four lamb shanks, 350–400 g (12–14 oz) each. Rub with the same butter, sear at 230°C (450°F) for 20 minutes, add 360 ml (1.5 cups) water or lamb stock, reduce to 160°C (325°F), and braise covered for 2.5 hours. The result is deeply gelatinous and saucy in a way the leg is not.

**Dairy-free version.** Replace butter with the same weight of coconut oil (refined, not virgin, which would overpower) or a good-quality non-dairy cooking block. The crust will be slightly thinner and the flavor less rich, but the spice logic holds.

**Stronger citrus note.** Add the juice of one orange directly to the pan when you lower the temperature, alongside the water. It brightens the sauce and cuts the fat in the finished jus.

## Storage and Reheating

Sliced cold lamb keeps in an airtight container in the refrigerator for three days. The fat will solidify around the meat; this is not a problem and actually protects it.

To reheat: arrange slices in a single layer in an oven-safe dish, spoon a little of the reserved pan jus over the top, cover tightly with foil, and warm at 160°C (325°F) for 15–18 minutes. Do not microwave a large cut like this; it dries the exterior before the center warms through.

The compound butter itself freezes well for up to two months. Roll it into a log in cling film and use it on roast vegetables, grilled flatbread, or rice.

## FAQ

### Can I use the same recipe for bone-in lamb chops or racks?
The rub works on rack of lamb, but the technique changes entirely. Racks are quick-roasting cuts; use the butter as a crust at 200°C (400°F) for 20–25 minutes depending on thickness. Do not apply the low-and-slow logic to a thin cut.

### What does semi-boneless actually mean, and why does it matter?
The hip and pelvic bones are removed for easier carving, while the shank bone is left for structural integrity and flavor. It is the default form most halal butchers produce for a roasting leg. If you receive a fully boned leg, tie it into a cylinder with kitchen twine and reduce total roasting time by 20–25 minutes.

### The butter is browning very quickly on the outside but the lamb is not yet done. What do I do?
Cover the pan loosely with foil from the moment you drop the temperature. The surface is already set from the high-heat phase; protecting it for the slow phase prevents any bitterness from over-browned butter solids.

### Is vadouvan authentically halal?
Vadouvan contains no haram ingredients, but as with any spice blend, check the label on the brand you purchase. Some blends use anti-caking agents processed on shared equipment. Roellinger's and most artisan producers have clean ingredient lists.

### Can I make this ahead for a dinner party?
Yes. Roast the lamb to 55°C (130°F) internal, then rest and refrigerate whole for up to one day. On the day, bring it to room temperature for an hour, then return it to a 160°C (325°F) oven covered for 30–35 minutes. It will finish cooking to the correct temperature without starting from raw. This is genuinely useful for entertaining.

### My pan juices are very salty. How do I fix them?
Add 60–120 ml of water or unsalted lamb stock and reduce briefly over medium heat on the stovetop. The salt concentration drops as the volume increases. You can also add a squeeze of orange juice, which provides enough acidity to balance the perception of saltiness.
