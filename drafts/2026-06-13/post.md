## Headnote

Scotch broth is one of those soups that exist to teach you something: that a single cheap cut, given enough water and time, becomes its own stock and its own meal simultaneously. The lamb shank is doing double duty here — the collagen-rich bone builds body in the broth while the meat turns from something tough into something you can pull apart with a spoon. Pearl barley thickens the liquid as it cooks without dissolving, keeping its shape and adding a mild nuttiness that works well against the slight bitterness of turnip. This is not a recipe that benefits from acceleration. You cannot pressure-cook your way to the same result, because part of what you are after is a broth that has stratified into something clean and savoury over three hours of quiet simmering. Start it in the late morning; eat it at lunch.

## Ingredients

### For the broth base

- 1 lamb shank (about 450 g / 1 lb), halal
- 2 tsp canola oil
- 1 litre (4 cups) cold water
- 800 ml (about 3½ cups) reduced-sodium halal beef or lamb stock
- 1 medium onion, halved
- 1 medium carrot, halved
- 1 celery rib, halved
- 2 whole cloves
- 1 bay leaf
- ¼ tsp dried rosemary, crushed between your fingers
- ¼ tsp dried thyme
- ¼ tsp whole black peppercorns
- 30 g (¼ cup) fresh flat-leaf parsley, minced

### For the soup

- 75 g (⅓ cup) medium pearl barley
- 200 g (1½ cups) turnip, peeled and cut into 1 cm (½ inch) julienne
- 2 medium carrots, coarsely chopped
- 1 medium leek, white part only, thinly sliced
- ¼ tsp fine salt
- ¼ tsp ground black pepper

## Ingredient notes

**Lamb shank — halal sourcing.** Any halal butcher will have these. A single shank of around 450 g gives you enough meat for four reasonable servings once stripped and returned to the pot. Do not try to substitute lamb shoulder chops here; the collagen in the shank is structural to the broth's body.

**Stock.** The original calls for beef broth. Halal beef stock works well; so does halal lamb stock, which makes the lamb flavour more pronounced. Avoid stock cubes with flavour enhancers if you can — the long simmer amplifies everything, including the metallic aftertaste some cubes leave.

**Pearl barley.** Supermarket standard. Rinse it under cold water before adding. It absorbs a significant volume of liquid as it cooks, so if your broth looks thin early on, resist the urge to add more water until the barley has cooked out fully.

**Turnip.** In Scottish cooking, what is called turnip is actually swede (rutabaga) — yellow-fleshed, dense, slightly sweet. In France and Pakistan, turnip is the white-fleshed shalgam. Either works here, though swede holds its shape better in a long soup. The flavour difference is real but not decisive.

**Whole cloves.** Two cloves pressed into the onion halves (bouquet garni style) is all this broth needs. More, and it starts to smell like biryani, which is not what you are going for.

## Method

1. **Sear the shank.** Heat the canola oil in a heavy pot over medium-high heat until it shimmers. Pat the lamb shank dry with paper towels — wet meat does not brown, it steams. Sear on all sides until a deep mahogany crust forms, about 3 to 4 minutes per side. This is not optional flavour; the Maillard reaction here is what gives the broth its depth. Lift the shank out and set aside.

2. **Build the broth base.** Press the two cloves into the cut faces of the onion halves. Add the onion, halved carrot, and halved celery rib to the same pot without additional fat. Let them sit on the hot surface for two minutes to pick up some colour from the fond left by the lamb. This is deglazing by vegetable, essentially.

3. **Add liquid and aromatics.** Return the lamb shank to the pot. Pour in the water and stock. Add the bay leaf, rosemary, thyme, and peppercorns. The liquid should just cover the shank; if it does not, add water in 125 ml increments until it does. Bring to a strong simmer, then immediately reduce heat to low.

4. **Skim.** For the first 20 minutes, check every 5 minutes and skim off the grey foam that rises. This is coagulated protein and fat. Removing it is the difference between a clear, clean broth and a cloudy, slightly muddy one. After 20 minutes the foaming largely stops.

5. **Simmer the shank.** Cook, partially covered, at a gentle simmer (you want occasional bubbles, not a rolling boil) for 1 hour 30 minutes to 2 hours. The meat is done when it pulls away from the bone easily when you prod it with a fork. Do not rush this.

6. **Extract and strain.** Lift the shank out carefully — it will be fragile. Set it aside to cool slightly. Pour the broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean pot, pressing on the vegetables to extract what they have. Discard the strained solids. You should have approximately 1 to 1.2 litres of broth.

7. **Pull the meat.** Once the shank is cool enough to handle, pull the meat off the bone in rough chunks. Discard the bone, any cartilage, and obvious fat. You want the lean, fall-apart meat pieces back in the soup.

8. **Cook the barley.** Bring the strained broth back to a simmer. Add the rinsed pearl barley. Cook uncovered for 20 minutes, stirring occasionally.

9. **Add the vegetables.** Add the turnip, chopped carrots, and sliced leek. Return the pulled lamb to the pot. Season with the salt and pepper. Simmer for a further 25 to 30 minutes, until the barley is fully tender and the vegetables have given up their rawness but are not falling apart.

10. **Finish.** Stir in the minced parsley. Taste and adjust salt. Ladle into deep bowls. The broth should be lightly viscous from the barley starch, not watery.

## Variations

**Bone-in neck chops instead of shank.** Cheaper and more widely available from halal butchers. The flavour is comparable; the meat yield per piece is lower, so use two or three chops. Sear them the same way.

**Add lentils for more body.** 50 g (¼ cup) of red lentils added with the barley will dissolve almost completely by the end, thickening the soup further and adding a background earthiness. This pushes it slightly toward a Pakistani dal-soup territory, which is not a bad place to be.

**Root vegetable swap.** Parsnip in place of or alongside turnip. It sweetens the broth more aggressively. Good in cold weather when you want something a little rounder.

**Lamb stock only, no beef.** If you prefer a purer lamb flavour and can find good halal lamb stock, use it entirely in place of the beef stock. The result is less rounded but more direct.

## Storage and reheating

Let the soup cool to room temperature, then refrigerate in a sealed container within two hours of cooking. It keeps for four days in the fridge. The barley will continue absorbing liquid overnight; the soup will be significantly thicker by the next day. Loosen it with a splash of water or stock when reheating. Reheat in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring, until it reaches a full simmer.

For freezing: freeze without the barley and vegetables if possible (i.e., freeze the strained broth and pulled meat only), then add fresh barley and vegetables when reheating. If you freeze the finished soup, accept that the barley and turnip will have a softer texture after thawing. Still good; just different.

## FAQ

### Can I make this in a pressure cooker to save time?

You can extract the lamb faster — roughly 45 minutes at high pressure instead of 90 minutes on the stove. The broth will be richer and slightly cloudier because the agitation of pressure cooking emulsifies fats that a gentle simmer would leave separate. It is a different result, not a worse one, but it is not the classic Scotch broth texture. If time is the constraint, do it.

### The broth looks pale and thin after straining. Did I do something wrong?

Not necessarily. The colour depends heavily on how hard you seared the shank and how much fond you developed. If the sear was good, the broth should be amber-brown. If it looks thin despite a good colour, it will thicken when the barley cooks in it. If it is genuinely watery, you can reduce it by a quarter before adding the barley.

### Do I need both water and stock, or can I use all stock?

All stock works and produces a more intensely flavoured broth. All water also works and produces something lighter. The half-and-half approach is deliberate: you are building a stock in the pot, and adding some pre-made stock gives it a head start without overwhelming the natural flavour of the shank.

### My barley is still chewy after 20 minutes. What happened?

Pearl barley cooking time varies by brand and age of the grain. Old barley (sitting in a pantry for a year) takes longer. If it is still chewy at 20 minutes, add the vegetables as scheduled and just cook everything for 10 to 15 minutes longer. The vegetables will be softer but the soup will not be ruined.

### Can I use lamb stock cubes instead of proper stock?

Yes, with caveats. Most stock cubes are high in sodium and contain flavour enhancers. Taste the broth before adding the final salt and pepper, and reduce or eliminate the added salt accordingly. The depth of flavour will be lower than a good liquid stock, but the soup will still be worthwhile.

### Is this soup meant to be thick or brothy?

Traditional Scotch broth sits between a thick stew and a clear soup — the barley and vegetables give it substance without making it opaque. If yours is too thick, thin with hot water. If too thin, a 10-minute uncovered simmer will reduce it. The final texture is a matter of preference, not correctness.
